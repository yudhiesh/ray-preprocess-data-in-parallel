Why Publicly Discussing My HIV-Positive Status Was the Best Decision I've Made

Not to mention the bravest.

By David Russell, as told to Corey Atad
Nov 30, 2016
Courtesy of David Russell

I often think about the responsibilities I have—the debt I owe, in a way—to those who came before me. I've been successful in my life, but I'm a gay man, living in 2016, in what will soon be Donald Trump's America. I'm also HIV-positive.

There's a responsibility in being HIV-positive, especially if you're going to be out about it. I take that seriously. I'm still stigmatized for my status. It can be, and remains, a difficult road to hope. At the same time, because this is 2016 and I have access to medication, my life is as normal as anyone else's.

I came out publicly with my HIV status earlier this year on Facebook, and in an interview at Poz.com. It was a long time coming. I was partly inspired by a friend, Shawn Decker, who wrote a book called My Pet Virus about being HIV-positive. He asked me speak about my status. Everyone in my life knew, and I was open and honest with them, but I had never spoken about it in a public way.

I'm a gay man, living in 2016, in what will soon be Donald Trump's America. I'm also HIV positive.

When Shawn asked me, I didn't even think about it. I just said, "Yes, I'll do it." I remembered so clearly how alienated I felt when I found out I was positive. As someone who is navigating the dating world, my status comes up over and over again. I looked at this as a failsafe; as a way of putting it out there so I wouldn't have to put it out there [over and over again] anymore. Afterwards, I was surprised by how many people were inspired to share their status with me, or publicly. It was one of the best things I've ever done.

I was diagnosed 14 years ago in 2002, not long after coming to Los Angeles. I was 26. Those early years were tough. I was certain my life was over. People often had mixed responses when I disclosed my status. I remember one date asking, "Why would you trick me?" In the beginning I felt maybe that those responses were just. There still is so much ignorance about HIV. By coming out publicly now, I'm looking to alleviate some of that ignorance.

Courtesy of David Russell

The fact is, having HIV is no longer a guaranteed death sentence. It's a manageable disease. I take one pill per day, I see a doctor regularly, and I take care of my body. I live normally, and some days I don't think about my disease at all. In some ways, HIV has actually made my life better. That may sound odd, and I certainly wouldn't wish that anyone contract HIV, but it hit the fast-forward button on my life. It forced me to look inward, and to love myself for who I am. Before my diagnosis, I hadn't considered my health much—I was more concerned with my whether I looked cute and young enough. After my diagnosis, I had to grow up much faster than I would have otherwise, and I had to develop a healthier relationship with my own body, and that has been a real positive in my life.

Having strong relationships is an important part of my healthier lifestyle, and that includes support from family and friends—even my doctor, whom I've been seeing since my diagnosis. These relationships help sustain me. At first I was embarrassed, and I would apologize to others for my status, even though it was something that had happened to me. A friend of mine once said to me, "Why are you apologizing to me? I'm sorry that you're having to go through this, and I love you." He told me that I should see a therapist, and I've been seeing the same therapist ever since. He has taught me that I don't need to apologize for who I am.

For gay men, our history is constantly being rewritten or told by other people, or written out completely.

For me, being HIV-positive—being out about it and not living in shame—led to becoming the most fully realized version of myself that I've ever been. For gay men, our history is constantly being rewritten or told by other people, or written out completely. I've seen the HIV/AIDS history relegated to side columns, and left out of the legacy of Ronald Reagan, so I appreciate the history of the stigma. But I want people to understand that HIV doesn't play a very large role in my life anymore. It's not the death sentence it once was: It's part of the background of my life.

That's what HIV is now, thanks to the strides we've made scientifically. It's the rest that needs to catch up.
